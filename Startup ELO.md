## Viral product loop

The viral loop should be intentionally tighter than a Crunchbase clone. A user should land on the site, see **one matchup**, answer **one question**, watch the Elo move, and then be prompted to **share the result card** or **submit a company that deserves to be in the system**. That is the high-frequency action. Everything else should support that loop rather than compete with it. This is the same general pattern that makes community curation products work: Product Hunt centers launches and public community discovery, Duolingo centers one competitive action inside a league system, and Open Graph-rich URLs let shared links travel as visual objects rather than dead text links. ## Ranking, moderation, and trust

For launch, you should use **three parallel public ratings**, not one universal score. The public product becomes much easier to understand if every table is clearly labeled: **Valuation**, **Growth**, and **Durability**. Under the hood, keep the math simple at first: separate Elo-style updates per question, with stage-aware K-factors and a provisional period for newly added companies. That buys simplicity without painting you into a corner. The important architectural decision is to store every individual comparison and voter segment from day one, so you can later re-estimate with BradleyŌĆōTerry or TrueSkill without rebuilding the product. You should assume that early comparison data will be **sparse and noisy**. Recent ranking research makes that explicit: pairwise methods are powerful, but distinguishing items confidently becomes harder when comparisons are limited or noisy. That is a strong reason to show users a visible confidence label such as **Provisional**, **Established**, or **Thin sample**, instead of pretending every rank is equally certain.
